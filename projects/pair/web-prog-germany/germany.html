About Berlin

Berlin is Germany's capital and most populous city. The city is located in the midst of the North German Plain, at the crossroads of an east-west commercial and geographic axis that helped it become the capital of the Kingdom of Prussia and, later, of a unified Germany, beginning in 1871. Berlin's past splendour faded in 1945, but the city survived World War II's destruction and was rebuilt to demonstrate incredible economic and cultural progress.

Landscape

Berlin lies in northeastern Germany, in a low-lying marshland forests region with mostly flat topography. It is located within the huge Northern European Plain, which runs from northern France to western Russia.

Tourism

Berlin is a city full of contrasts. On the one hand, there are reminders of its stormy past all over the place. But it's also a contemporary megalopolis—nine times the size of Paris in terms of area—with fascinating new attractions and a revolutionary spirit. It is a place unlike any other because of this juxtaposition. Berlin, an artistic hub with a bevy of galleries and odd installations, has turned the final remaining portions of the Berlin Wall into a permanent open-air display.

History

The capital of Germany, Berlin, was founded in the 13th century. The city's Holocaust monument and the graffitied ruins of the Berlin Wall serve as reminders of the city's stormy 20th-century history. Its 18th-century Brandenburg Gate, which was divided during the Cold War, has become a symbol of reconciliation.

Culture of Berlin

German culture is distinguished by its inhabitants, dialect, and customs. It has played a significant role in the history of Europe, as well as other parts of the world. It is known as Germany by English speakers and Deutschland by Germans. It is also known for their great number of talented poets and thinkers.

Architecture

The country, having been through a turbulent history, reflects its rich and diverse architecture. The story of Germany is best told through its palaces, castles, churches, and monuments. Ancient architecture and civilization flourished throughout the land that is now Germany, including amphitheaters, spas, and roman bridges.

Cuisine

Germans consume a lot of beer, eat a lot of sausages, and bake a variety of breads. This isn't just a stereotype; it's a fact. The average German consumes roughly 140 liters of beer per year, according to estimates. In only one year, they consumed 2.25 liters of beer, the lowest record since the collapse of the Berlin Wall in 1990. After the Czech Republic, they are Europe's second-largest beer consumers.

Celebrations

Despite their reputation as a bureaucratic people, the Germans know how to have a good time. This notion is best demonstrated by the large crowds that attend their festivities. Both types of events occur during a happy time of year where entire cities throw all-out celebrations and colorful festivals.

Festivals of Berlin

Berlin being a major art hub, it is no secret that berlin is home to various festivals celebrating various art forms. From festivals celebrating films, music, and even history. There is no shortage of fun events to attend when you're in Berlin.

BERLINALE

Date: February 10-20

A world-renowned film festival held in Berlin annually. The event shows over more than 400 international films, and serves as a hub where talents from all over the globe gather.

STADTLESEN

Date: April 21-24

In the heart of Berlin, this unique reading festival offers open book shelves. With over 3,000 volumes of all kinds and cozy furnishings great for cuddling up in. StadtLesen converts a public plaza into a quiet refuge for reading.

CLASSIC OPEN AIR

Date: July 7-11

The Classic Open Air Festival is set in the great landscape of the Gendarmenmarkt, one of Berlin's most attractive squares in the historical center.
